People who experience chemical burns and other eye injuries may develop limbal stem cell deficiency, an irreversible loss of cells on the tissue surrounding the cornea. These patients experience permanent vision loss, pain and discomfort in the affected eye. Without limbal cells and a healthy eye surface, patients are unable to undergo artificial cornea transplants, the current standard of vision rehabilitation.

So my understanding is that the cornea isn't exactly "alive" anymore. As a guess, you would be better off wondering if there are chemicals getting into the vitreous that cause keratoconus and if those chemicals can in some way be mitigated.

Very unlikely. This is specifically about treating limbal stem cell deficiency, to which keratoconus seems to be unrelated. Furthermore, it is specifically about treating unilateral LSCD in one eye by transplanting stem cells from the other, healthy eye. Meaning, it doesn't apply when both eyes are afflicted. Thirdly, the treatment helped patients to meet the preconditions for a corneal transplant, which means it's no substitute for a corneal transplant. This isn't a magical "regrow my cornea" treatment.

Maybe the two last paragraphs of the introduction section of the study help? Apparently, yes, a stem cell based therapy was a thing already but it wasn't allowed in the USA, so they made a manufacturing process that didn't involve murine (mice) cell lines.

https://www.science.org/doi/10.1126/sciadv.adg6470

> CLET is not available in the United States, as the manufacturing procedure uses xenogeneic murine feeder cells and fetal bovine serum (FBS) and is not compatible with current good manufacturing practices (cGMPs). The use of murine fibroblasts carry a risk of viral contaminants and inflammatory response against murine antigens

> We developed a manufacturing technique to address these shortcomings: cultivated autologous limbal epithelial cells (CALECs). We removed antibiotics and murine feeder cells, used highly reproducible methods for LSC isolation and expansion onto human amniotic membrane, and developed rigorous quality criteria for the manufactured tissue graft.
