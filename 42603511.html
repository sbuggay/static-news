While this seems true and I hope these companies get a nasty fine and some regulatory action takes place to wipe out these third party collusion apps, I'm kind of surprised at the corporate learned helplessness here.

Back in the day McDonald's or one of their fast food competitors would have built their own frozen potato pipeline, made a massive marketing gimmick about cheapest fries and shattered this cartel inside a year. It sounds like the companies are taking a high margin and the farmers would love to sell to anyone else. But it feels like the current managers at these fast food companies have gotten so used to outsourcing every part of production they lack the knowledge/remit to even try to set up a competing supply line.

It's only efficient to focus your core competencies if the people in charge of those stay smaller. This seems to lead to a weird outcome where everyone just consolidates as much as they can to allow more bargaining power. Eventually the whole market is what filled with minimally competent firms who can do nothing but one thing and negotiate that one thing to high margins. This feels like a silly outcome.

Companies used to be more flexible but maybe when you have a multi-billion dollar supply chain and complex contract structure you can't just learn to do something new to solve a problem anymore.

The small players can't do much. As mentioned in the article, either potatoes and DIYing are cheaper or they aren't, but medium to large firms presumably could/should do something.
