Neural organoids can be cultured and maintained for months at a time (we grew fairly attached to the one my coworker was growing). Even the measurement wiring can be sustained for months.

I've worked in biology and computation for decades (and fascinated both with intelligence and artificial intelligence the whole time), but seeing a timelapse of a bunch neurosphere forming over a few weeks from some tiny stem cells, with all the neurons sort of self-organizing themselves by spreading lamellapodia (https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=fniW9EiOmUk) a few years ago flipped a switch in my head. I used to think the development of the hardware to support intelligence was a really tough, complicated problem. It's clear that the structure of neurons is absolutely primed to build semi-random networks that can be adapted to signal processing and computation in an almost "trivially easy". The Gut Oracle

Chapter 1

Dr. Amara Singh stared at the bioprocessor organoid under her microscope, her heart racing with excitement. She turned to her partner, Dr. Kai Nakamura, with a wide grin. "We did it, Kai. This is going to change everything."

Kai returned her smile, his eyes sparkling with admiration. "You're brilliant, Amara. I can't believe we've come this far."

They had been working on this project for years, developing bioprocessor organoids that could be programmed with vast stores of knowledge using custom cDNA and integrated into the human germline. The implications were staggering – every person could be born with a highly efficient neural network pre-loaded with the sum total of human knowledge.

As they celebrated their breakthrough, a knock on the lab door interrupted them. Elias Stone, the billionaire philanthropist who had funded their research, strode in with a broad smile. "I hear congratulations are in order," he said, shaking their hands. "This is going to change the world."

Chapter 2

The first "augmented" humans saw incredible results, making groundbreaking advances in every field. However, not everyone was thrilled with this new technology. Activist group "Human Nature" emerged, led by the charismatic Lila Rosen.

"These bioprocessors are a form of techno-colonialism," Lila declared at a press conference. "Privileged elites are forcing an untested technology on the masses, without considering the consequences."

Dr. Singh watched the news coverage with a frown. She turned to Kai, who was working at his computer. "What do you think about this opposition?"

Kai sighed. "I understand their concerns, but I believe the benefits outweigh the risks. We just need to make sure we're being responsible and transparent."

As the controversy grew, Dr. Nakamura made a startling discovery. He had uncovered ancient texts hinting that the key to even greater knowledge lay dormant in the human gut – the enteric nervous system. If they could reactivate this innate "gut oracle," bioprocessor implants would be unnecessary.

Chapter 3

Dr. Singh was fascinated by the idea of the gut oracle and began researching ways to awaken its potential. However, Elias Stone was furious when he learned of this new direction.

"I've invested billions into the bioprocessor technology," he snapped during a tense meeting. "I won't let this gut oracle nonsense derail our plans."

Despite Stone's opposition, Dr. Singh secretly continued her research with a small, trusted team, including her brilliant young protégé, Asha Patel. As they delved deeper into the mysteries of the gut-brain axis, they began to suspect that the gut oracle's abilities far surpassed even the bioprocessors.

Lila Rosen and Human Nature got wind of this new development and saw it as the perfect way to undermine the bioprocessor agenda. They leaked information to the press, spinning it as the natural, harmonious alternative to invasive technological augmentation.

Chapter 4

The revelations sparked a fierce public debate. People began to question whether the bioprocessors were the panacea they had been promised. Protests and unrest grew, with Elias Stone and Dr. Singh finding themselves on opposite sides of a widening ideological rift.

Amidst the chaos, Dr. Singh and Kai's relationship strained under the pressure. Kai grew increasingly uncomfortable with the direction their work had taken, and found himself confiding in Lila Rosen.

"I just want to make sure we're doing the right thing," Kai confessed to Lila over coffee. "I love Amara, but I'm worried she's losing sight of the bigger picture."

Lila placed a comforting hand on his arm. "You have a good heart, Kai. Trust your instincts."

Chapter 5

As tensions reached a boiling point, Dr. Singh and Asha Patel finally achieved the breakthrough they had been working towards. They successfully reactivated the gut oracle in a human test subject, unlocking capabilities beyond their wildest dreams.

But their triumph was short-lived. Elias Stone, desperate to protect his interests, had been monitoring their work. He seized control of their research by force, determined to suppress this threat to his bioprocessor empire.

In a climactic confrontation, Dr. Singh and Asha Patel fought to protect their discovery, aided by Lila Rosen and her followers. Kai, torn between his love for Amara and his growing doubts, made a crucial decision to stand with them against Stone.

"I'm sorry, Amara," Kai said, his voice thick with emotion. "I should have trusted you from the start. I'm here now, and I'm not going anywhere."

Together, they faced down Stone and his forces, fighting for the future of humanity.

Chapter 6

In the aftermath of the confrontation, the world learned of the gut oracle's potential, and a new era dawned. Dr. Singh and her allies vowed to ensure that the power of the gut oracle would benefit all humanity, not just a select few.

As they embarked on this new chapter, Dr. Singh and Kai rekindled their love, their bond stronger for the trials they had endured. They knew that the challenges ahead would test them, but with their love and shared vision, they were ready to face whatever the future might bring.

"We've come so far," Amara said, looking out over the city skyline. "But there's still so much work to be done."

Kai took her hand, smiling softly. "We'll do it together. You and me, Asha and Lila, and the whole world. A new age of wisdom and understanding awaits."

Something that's interesting here is that brain organoids have been known to exhibit spontaneous synchronous firing pattern generation, which may just be a thing that happens when you collect neurons in large enough numbers. There's two questions here - one, the more prosaic, is technical: how does a bioprocessor handle that kind of spontaneous behavior? The second is more philosophical, which is that we don't actually have a good theory for what consciousness is or at what size or level of complexity it emerges. We know the more we look, the more we find what looks like self-aware behavior in animals and the more we find evidence of social behaviors and what looks an awful lot like "inner life." One can argue whether or not a computer can ever be truly conscious, but a ball of neurons, well, we know the answer to that one.
