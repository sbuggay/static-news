We had a literary theory seminar at college, and I read this text for the first time therein. The arguments for a reading of a historical difference between the "translations" falls apart when you show that the "history" is constructed therein: and such was my reading, Borges is making a magic trick which makes you think that there is such a thing as historical difference by inventing an impossible condition where you think the only reason the two different texts are different is because two different people wrote them, even though Borges authored neither.

Here's an interesting tidbit about Jorge Luis Borges that may have inspired him to write that story. First, some backstory:

  "Under his grandmother's tutelage, Borges learned to read English before he could read Spanish. [...] In Borges's autobiographical essay, he recalled reading even the great Spanish masterpiece, Cervantes's Don Quixote, in English before reading it in Spanish."
The funny thing is, when he finally came across a copy of Don Quixote in its original Spanish... he dismissed it as "a bad translation" of the book he had read in English.
