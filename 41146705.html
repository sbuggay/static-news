I'm not fond of these articles that hype up the 0.1% - 0.01% earners of unskilled jobs. Simply for the fact that it gives a very wrong picture of how well you can earn, and second - the more you earn, the HARDER the job is. Physically.

Your pay is directly proportional to how dangerous and demanding the job is.

As a student, I used to work as a longshoreman whenever needed. The pay was phenomenal - as in $500-$1000 a day. Some of the guys that worked full time would pull $10000-$15000 / month. Everyone knew about this job, but the realty was:

- High paced backbreaking work 12-24 hours a day (you'd unload 20-50 kg blocks of frozen fish from the refrigeration room on large industrial trawlers)

- Working in really terrible conditions. Freezing cold, lots of noise, always on the lookout for falling objects.

The only reason I could work there, was because someone got injured - and you always had injured people there.

The oldest guy working there was in his mid 30s. By the time you were pushing 40, you'd be too injured or slow to work there. The lucky few were placed to operate the ship crane.

But the pay was good, probably the best in town as far as unskilled work goes.
