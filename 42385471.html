I have read King's novel, and I have watched Kubrick's movie (many, many times). I am not usually a friend of novel adaptations, but the movie simply is in another league than the book. It is much, much better. King's novel is a relatively straightforward horror story / thriller, with some elements that go a little bit over the top. Kubrick's movie is a postmodern masterpiece, with layers upon layers upon layers of meaning. After 20 years of re-watching both of them I have come to the conclusion that Shining cuts even deeper than 2001. And it also works as an entertaining (and frightening) horror movie. In my opinion, it is Kubrick's singular masterpiece. And, above all, it is still quite faithful to the original novel. Many dialogues from the book appear virtually unchanged in the screenplay.

That being said, I understand why King the author doesn't like it. Kubrick carved something out of this novel which really wasn't there in the first place. To King, this may have appeared as an arrogant (or even condescending) abuse of his story.

The Shining and the Power of Narrative

https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=EBFnZu-VYlQ

Summary and comparison of the article and youtube video analysis by Claude.

This analysis focuses on the narrative elements and symbolism in "The Shining," particularly the role of stories and storytelling, which interestingly complements the previous analysis about spatial impossibilities. Let me summarize the key points and connect them:

Key Points from This Analysis: 1. The Scrapbook's Significance - Though barely acknowledged in the film (unlike King's novel), the scrapbook represents the hotel's history and narrative power - Its subtle presence aligns with the film's broader theme of stories consuming their readers/viewers

2. Narrative Consumption - The film is filled with references to stories and storytelling (Donner Party, TV shows, ghost stories) - Characters are portrayed as both readers and writers of narratives - Wendy is specifically characterized as a horror film fan (Kubrick's addition)

3. Native American Subtext - The hotel's location on Native burial grounds - Connection to the Wendigo myth and colonial violence - The film opens with tribal music and landscape shots, suggesting a larger historical narrative

4. Power of Narrative Control - The hotel's ghosts (Lloyd, Grady) move from being subjects of Jack's writing to controlling him - Jack becomes trapped within the hotel's narrative rather than writing his own - The hotel itself becomes the true storyteller, consuming its characters

Connections to Previous Analysis: 1. Intentional Disorientation - The spatial impossibilities from the first analysis mirror this analysis's focus on narrative confusion - Both create a sense of being lost (physically and narratively) - The maze serves as a metaphor in both: physically impossible spaces and narrative labyrinth

2. Kubrick's Control - First analysis showed how Kubrick deliberately created impossible spaces - This analysis reveals how he deliberately subverted King's original narrative - Both demonstrate Kubrick's meticulous manipulation of the viewer's experience

3. Layers of Reality - The spatial analysis showed how rooms and corridors couldn't physically exist as shown - This analysis reveals how narratives within the film similarly overlap and contradict - Both aspects contribute to the film's unsettling atmosphere

4. Movement Patterns - First analysis noted how Kubrick moves "laterally" through the hotel rather than vertically - This analysis connects that to storytelling, noting how the narrative similarly moves sideways through different stories rather than deeper into any one tale

The combination of these analyses suggests that Kubrick was creating a deliberately disorienting experience on multiple levels - both spatial and narrative - to reinforce the hotel's supernatural power and its ability to trap and consume its inhabitants. The impossible architecture serves the same purpose as the overlapping narratives: to create a sense of being lost in a space that operates outside normal rules of reality.
