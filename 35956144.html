> The maneuvering thruster system sounds similar to that of the Harrier VTOL.

It doesn't really, to me. The big idea here is using blown air to avoid having physical surfaces deflect-- neither the engine nozzle or any actual control surfaces on the plane. The Harrier uses fairly conventional bending nozzles to vector trust, and uses fairly conventional control surfaces at speed.

> That system was very difficult for humans to fly.

Anything helicopter-like is difficult to fly, and the Harrier is a pretty mediocre "helicopter" with relatively poor response times. In principle blown-air deflection of thrust could be more responsive for VTOL-type aircraft, but that's not even what this is aiming at.

(The deflection attained of thrust is not sufficient for VTOL use at this point-- nor is deflecting thrust in one degree of freedom from one point sufficient for hovering flight.)

> Pure-thrust vehicles are unstable.

Blowing air to adjust airflow at the trailing edge doesn't necessarily produce anything too hard to control-- it's pretty similar to conventional control schemes. On the other hand, this aircraft will not be controllable at all without the active systems gating the airflow, while aircraft with control surfaces can often fallback completely to dumb control cables.

carabiner|root|parent|prev|next|

The key concept is Coanda effect: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coand%C4%83_effect

There's a diagram of how this is implemented in the aircraft: https://www.baesystems.com/en/article/magma-the-future-of-fl...

For roll, air is blown out of the TE aftward. Per Coanda and the lift equation (lift is proportional to square of flow speed), this blowing will generate higher lift on one wing, imparting a roll moment on the aircraft. For pitch, air is blown up at the engine's exhaust surface to point the flow up/down. It is shaped so that with 0 blowing, it's pointing down.

thunderbird120|parent|prev|next|

Not really, thrust vectoring is separate from what they're doing here. Thrust vectoring allows you to literally use thrust from the engine to push the plane in different directions, this is a bit more subtle. Basically you can use little puffs of air to control the flow of air over the wings and other lifting surfaces and this can be used to increase or decrease lift in certain places. The actual force produced by these little air jets it totally negligible but by changing the way air travels over the lifting surfaces it can change the lift generated, allowing you to get rid of moving mechanical control surfaces. This requires very careful engineering and a very detailed computer modeling to get right which is why it's only really happening now.
