I love making web apps where users bring their own keys. This approach combines the best of both worlds: the convenience of distributing executable files and the benefits of open source. So far, I have developed two web apps:

1. A live transcription and translation app that uses microphone input. This is useful for watching proprietary content and facilitating communication.

2. An app that translates SRT subtitles into various languages.

I opt for the "bring your own keys" model for two main reasons:

1. Low maintenance: As a professional software developer, I already maintain a lot of software, and the last thing I want is to maintain my side projects. My goal is to write and distribute these apps so they continue working without requiring constant attention from me.

2. Low cost: This model allows me to distribute the apps without ads. By having users provide their own keys, I can keep operational costs down and avoid the need for monetization through advertising.

This approach enables me to create and share useful tools while keeping both my maintenance burden and user costs to a minimum.

Depends how you do this. If you allow users to configure their own key, they basically end up using https to communicate the API key directly to the party that issued it. ellellem|root|parent|next|

Here's a more accurate breakdown:

1. TLS and Its Vulnerabilities: TLS/SSL stripping: This is a technique used by attackers to downgrade an HTTPS connection to HTTP, thereby removing the encryption. If an attacker can perform a successful TLS stripping attack, simply "using HTTPS" won't protect your data, as the connection would no longer be encrypted.

Fake Certificates: An attacker might present a fake certificate that your browser mistakenly trusts (e.g., through a compromised Certificate Authority or if you ignore a browser warning), allowing the attacker to intercept and decrypt what you think is an HTTPS connection.

2. Why "Always Use HTTPS" Alone Isn't Enough: In a Compromised Environment: If the attacker has managed to manipulate your connection, "using HTTPS" might give a false sense of security. If the attack involves SSL stripping or the use of a rogue certificate, your data could still be intercepted and read in plain text.

3. Better Protections Against MITM in Public WiFi: Use a VPN: A Virtual Private Network (VPN) creates a secure, encrypted tunnel between your device and the VPN server. Even if the attacker intercepts the traffic on the local network, it remains encrypted and unreadable due to the VPN. Verify Certificates: Be vigilant about certificate warnings. If your browser warns you about an untrusted certificate, don’t ignore it, especially on public WiFi. This could be a sign of a MITM attack.

HTTPS Everywhere: Browser extensions like HTTPS Everywhere force the use of HTTPS on sites that support it, but it’s not foolproof if the attacker is actively stripping SSL.

Check for HSTS (HTTP Strict Transport Security): Sites that use HSTS protect against some forms of SSL stripping because they enforce the use of HTTPS even on initial connections.

Disable Trust for Public Networks: Be cautious on public WiFi. Turn off automatic connection features, and avoid performing sensitive transactions unless you have additional protections like a VPN.

4. Advanced Security Measures: DNSSEC: Ensures that the DNS responses you receive are authentic and haven’t been tampered with. This can help prevent DNS spoofing. Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA): Protects your accounts even if your credentials are intercepted.

To sum it up, while "Always Use HTTPS" is generally good advice, it doesn't cover situations where TLS is compromised or bypassed. In scenarios involving potential MITM attacks, stronger measures like using a VPN, being cautious with certificates, and employing additional security practices are crucial.
