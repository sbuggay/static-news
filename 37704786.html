Note "pyrolysis" and "gasification" processes like this are the fast breeder reactor of non-nuclear energy. There are plenty of reports from the 1970s that pyrolysis could be a cheap way to create cheap electricity from "clean coal" or biomass or even create fuels.

There are numerous variations such as attempting to convert municipal waste to hydrogen-CO "syngas" with a plasma torch or attempting to cook plastic down to a toxic and carcinogenic mess of aromatic compounds that can be used as diesel fuel but the scenario is usually: (1) environmentalists tried to stop the project because of the toxics produced, (2) environmentalists fail to stop the project, they (3) build the machine, but (4) can't really get the machine to work and shut it down not long after it is put into operation.

See

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plasma_gasification_commercial...

https://www.theguardian.com/environment/2018/mar/02/clean-co...

It would be nice to see something succeed in this space but boy am I skeptical. Boosters make the point that it was a proven technology back in the 1910s, but a big coal gas factory they built in my hometown (Manchester, NH) became a big superfund site that is a huge gash in the area between the downtown and the river that I don't believe is fully remediated to this day.

Probably the most successful facility of this sort is

https://www.dakotagas.com/

but that project was really too big to fail and it took them about 25 years to get the bugs out and it get it financially sustainable after it went bankrupt. That project also has a big strip mine across the street that gives it quality feedstock that lets them run the process consistently. Throw in random plastic from municipal waste that will never be the same twice and getting production of any kind of product will be a struggle, never mind production of quality products.

The SASOL corporation of South Africa has long been using technology of this sort to make gasoline from coal when they were under embargo, it is proposed that they'll export the technology whenever oil prices spike, but they always seem to forgot what happened last time. SASOL was the worst performing major stock in the world in 2020.

Yes. Basically for this to be economical, it would have to be both low cost and more high in value than alternative ways to recycle plastic. Basically making plastic out of plastic probably takes less energy and results in a more valuable product that can be recycled more than once. Alternatively, burning it and using the resulting heat for energy generation is also very common.

And then there is the question of scale and sustainability. Recovering hydrogen from what is essentially a product made from fossil fuels to then burn it is not really that helpful. And there is only so much plastic out there that we can recover. At best we get a second use out of the fossil carbon before it is released back into the atmosphere. But you could argue that a landfill at least captures the carbon more permanently. So, labeling this as green or sustainable would be a bit of a stretch of the definition.

I'm not a chemist, so this is a very low knowledge post, but the carbon/hydrogen link in carbohydrates seems to be a rather weak one, we reconfigure it all the time on the long and winding process paths from crude to the many, many things we create from it (these days, pretty much everything that isn't metal or concrete).

The majority of the energy we use from burning carbohydrates comes not from oxidating carbon but from oxidating hydrogen: oxidating carbon does release slightly more energy per atom than oxidating hydrogen, but the hydrogen atoms outnumber the carbon ones by far. On the atom level, our society already is mostly hydrogen powered, but unfortunately limited to hydrogen packaged in carbon and we can't burn them separately.

With this technology, if it scales (big if, no question about that!), we can burn the oxygen for energy while retaining the carbon as stable, even somewhat useful (perhaps not in the amounts post-scaling...) solids that are relatively easy to keep out of the atmospheric cycle. If that really works, it will be a great improvement over what we do and certainly worth spending some of the net energy we'd get from the hydrogen on. And even if the net energy would be zero it could still be worth it, run the plant only at times of intermittent sources surplus. At zero energy balance it would be a 100$ efficiency storage solution (that would solve some of our plastic waste problems on the side)
