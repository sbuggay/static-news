I'm okay with conjuring (r+ix)(r+iy)(r+iz) out of thin air. I am also okay with adding an extra step at the bottom to make it a proof of Heron's formula, even though at its heart it's a proof of the radius of the incircle—because the former sounds way more cool.

However, a complete proof really should explain the diagram at the top to properly define x, y and z.

The idea, if I understand correctly, is to draw the altitudes of the three triangles AIB, BIC, CIA, and then show that the six resulting right triangles are congruent in pairs because they have one equal angle at A/B/C (the segments AI, BI, CI bisect the angles of the triangle ABC), and the hypotenuse in common. Not too complicated but even if a picture is worth a thousand words, it wouldn't hurt to have it written also in words.

A subsequent step would prove that r is equal for all pairs of triangles, because it is a common side of triangles coming from different pairs. (As a bonus this proves that the incenter is indeed the center of the inscribed circle).

Likewise, going from r+ix to u*e^iα is basically Pythagoras's theorem, but it would not hurt to write that explicitly. And it does not hurt to write "let s=(a+b+c)/2=x+y+z be the semiperimeter of the triangle" (x+y+z is not the definition of the semiperimeter!) and to only introduce it after x+y+z actually occurs somewhere.

It's a really cool proof, and hey it was written by a high school student—but the proof writing is as important as the proof IMO.
